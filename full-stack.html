The Stack is Getting Fuller

2024-10-08

I have spent most of my career in tech primarily focusing on backend related implementations. From pioneering machine learning research on entity resolution to architecting massive distributed systems and developing cutting-edge data engineering software, my expertise has spanned a wide range of complex backend technologies. Recently, however, I have been jumping a bit into frontend development diving headfirst into building a full platform UI for my company as well as a "modern" landing page with React, Material-UI (MUI), and Tailwind CSS.

My first encounter with web development dates back to 2011 when I was contracted to build a company website using the inaugural release of Twitter Bootstrap. At the time, it felt revolutionary—a toolkit that simplified the creation of responsive designs without starting from scratch. Of course I didn't fully understand what all of that was at the time and the site I built looked like shit but looking back, it's astounding to see how far we've come. The evolution of frontend frameworks and tools has been nothing short of remarkable, opening up a world of possibilities that were once unimaginable. I want to go through a few things I've taken away from some of the work I've done over the past few weeks.

One of the most exhilarating aspects of frontend development is the instant gratification it provides. Unlike backend work, where changes often require complex deployments or extensive testing before results are visible, frontend tweaks manifest immediately. This rapid feedback loop is addictive. It presents a unique challenge: the constant urge to tweak and refine. This "tweaker's dilemma" is both a blessing and a curse.

I have been leveraging the integration of generative AI tools like Cursor way more in learning frontend development than I have in the past. It has been a crucial tool for seeing how the different components of a React UI interact and are structured. Being able to perform simple operations such as "move this button here" or "make this component cleaner" has freed up so much development time while tweaking the design of a page.

However, AI tools have their limitations, particularly when it comes to visual design and user experience. The ability to create intuitive, aesthetically pleasing interfaces often requires human creativity and empathy—a reminder that certain aspects of design remain inherently human. Asking Claude to make a page more visually appealing often makes it uglier and less functional. Maybe design will be my next area of focus.

Frontend development presents an overwhelming array of options for achieving any given result. This abundance of choice is paralyzing. There are an insane amount of frameworks and libraries that all perform relatively similar outcomes. Navigating these choices has been a significant learning curve, especially compared to the more established conventions in backend development. It's a journey of continuous learning and adaptation. Thankfully generative AI will just choose a library and will often get one that works in now or two tries.

The UIs I’ve built recently are wild compared to the one I originally built almost a decade and a half ago. Instead of some poorly formatted bootstrap elements I now have data piping in from APIs, interactive WebGL objects, customizable charts/graphs, and a much better style. I'm not fully there yet but I can say that the road to full stack is getting fuller.
